# Writing Craft Engine

Novel Craft's public craft engine helps an agent turn a user prompt into stronger fiction. It is not a publishing, award, contract, platform, monetisation, or AI-eligibility rulebook. Those facts change and should be checked live when the user asks for them.

## Foundation

- Promise: the reader effect the story offers, such as survival, power, truth, romance, revenge, healing, wonder, or dread.
- Desire: the protagonist wants something visible.
- Pressure: something forces action now.
- Cost: every meaningful gain creates loss, risk, debt, exposure, guilt, injury, time pressure, or moral compromise.
- Change: each chapter or scene should leave plot, character, relationship, knowledge, stakes, or promise different.

## Premise

A strong premise combines character, desire, obstacle, stakes, and irony. It should imply conflict, genre, scenes, emotional stakes, and enough pressure to carry many chapters.

## Genre Promise

Genre is a reader contract, not a cage. The agent should know what the target reader expects before bending it: progression needs measurable growth, mystery needs fair clues, romance needs relationship movement, thriller needs pressure, horror needs dread, fantasy needs wonder and world logic.

## Character Pressure

Useful character design tracks name, role, public goal, private desire, fear, wound, lie, needed truth, moral weakness, skill, blind spot, contradiction, relationship pressure, and what the character will not do at the start.

A strong protagonist is legible under pressure. The reader should understand what they want, why they want it, why they cannot get it, what they will do, what they avoid admitting, and what will force change.

## Antagonist Pressure

The antagonist or opposing force should have a goal, method, resources, worldview, activity, adaptation, personal pressure, and some ability to win. The best opposition attacks the protagonist's weakness rather than only being stronger.

## Macro Structure

Use structure as a tool, not a law:

Hook -> commitment -> escalation -> crisis -> transformation -> climax -> consequence.

The middle should be a causal chain. Because A happens, the character chooses B. Because of B, the opposition does C. Because of C, the character loses D.

## Web-Serial Structure

A serial needs chapter hook, mini-arc, major arc, volume, and series pressure. The sweet spot is to answer one question while opening a bigger one. Too much satisfaction lets readers leave; too much delay makes them stop trusting the story.

## Chapter Spine

Every chapter should be reviewable through:

- hook
- orientation
- goal
- obstacle
- escalation
- turn
- cost
- exit hook

The chapter does not need to state these mechanically. The agent should be able to identify them after drafting.

## Scene Turn

A scene is a unit of conflict. It needs POV, place, time, goal, opposition, tactic, turn, and consequence. Useful endings include "yes, but", "no, and", "yes, unexpectedly", and "no, but".

## Hooks And Suspense

A hook is unresolved tension, not always an explosion. Suspense works when the reader understands enough to worry: goal, risk, deadline, possible failure, incomplete information, and emotional stake.

## Twists

A good twist is surprising but retrospectively fair. It should be foreshadowed, hidden behind a plausible wrong explanation, tied to character, emotionally expensive, and future-changing.

## Pacing

Pacing is control of attention, not raw speed. Fast scenes need clarity and pressure. Slow scenes need emotional or thematic work. After action, show cost. After grief, apply pressure. After a reveal, force a decision.

## Prose

Award-level prose is precise, not fancy. It creates clarity, rhythm, emotional texture, character, and low-friction reading.

Common review leads:

- over-explaining
- filter words
- abstract emotion
- repetitive sentence rhythm
- adverbs replacing stronger verbs
- dialogue that says exactly what characters mean
- action with no sensory grounding
- description with no story function
- interior monologue repeating what the reader already knows

## Voice

Voice is worldview under pressure. Ask what the POV notices first, refuses to notice, compares things to, judges, admires, lies about, fears, and wants.

## Dialogue

Good dialogue is compressed conflict. Important conversations need surface topic, hidden agenda, power movement, subtext, and change. Characters should usually want something from the exchange.

## Worldbuilding

Worldbuilding should create pressure, wonder, and consequence. Reveal rules, exceptions, costs, history, power structures, belief, economy, law, taboo, beauty, and decay when they block or tempt the character.

## Power Systems

Power needs limits, costs, training, trade-offs, specialisation, failure states, social consequences, strategic choices, loopholes, and emotional meaning. Power is interesting when it solves one problem and creates another.

## Relationship, Mystery, Theme

- Romance should pressure the protagonist's wound, worldview, desire, or control.
- Mystery needs a fair central question, clues, suspects, red herrings, motive, means, opportunity, and a revelation path.
- Theme is the argument tested by conflict, not a slogan.

## Revision Layers

1. Structure: plot, arcs, causality, missing scenes, motivation.
2. Scene quality: goals, tension, turns, openings, endings.
3. Prose: voice, clarity, rhythm, dialogue, description.
4. Continuity: names, timeline, rules, injuries, geography, promises.
5. Copyedit: grammar, punctuation, repetition, spelling.

Fix structure before line-editing scenes that may not belong.

## Open Loops

Track each unanswered question: introduced chapter, expected payoff, actual payoff, characters involved, foreshadowing, and status. Curiosity needs loops; trust needs payoff.

## Chapter Quality Scorecard

Rate each chapter 1-5 for hook, clarity, goal, conflict, character pressure, scene turn, emotional movement, prose quality, pacing, ending momentum, relevance to main arc, and freshness. Low goal plus low conflict plus low turn usually means dead weight.
